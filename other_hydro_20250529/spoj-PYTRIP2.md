<p>
Pythagoras is credited, by tradition, for the first proof of the relation
<strong><em>a</em><sup>2</sup> + <em>b</em><sup>2</sup> = <em>c</em><sup>2</sup></strong>
 in any right angled triangle where <strong><em>c</em></strong> is hypotenuse and <strong><em>a</em></strong> and <strong><em>b</em></strong> are the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathetus">catheti</a>.
</p>
<p>
We define a Pythagorean triple as a set of three positive integers <strong><em>a</em></strong>, <strong><em>b</em></strong>, and <strong><em>c</em></strong> which satisfy the above equation , ie ,
<strong><em>a</em><sup>2</sup> + <em>b</em><sup>2</sup> = <em>c</em><sup>2</sup></strong>. <br>
{3,4,5} is the most common example of such triples.
</p>

<h3>Input</h3>
<p>The first line of input contains an integer
 <strong><em>T</em></strong>, the number of test cases.</p>

<p> Each of the next <strong><em>T</em></strong> lines contains
 two integers <strong><em>N</em></strong>, <strong><em>M</em></strong>.</p>

<h3>Output</h3>
<p>For each test case, print on a single line the number of Pythagorean triplet <strong><em>{a,b,c}</em></strong>
 such that <strong><em>N ¡Ü a,b,c ¡Ü M</em></strong>.</p>

<h3>Example</h3>
<pre><strong>Input:</strong>
3
1 5
4 10
10 100

<strong>Output:</strong>
1
1
45
</pre>
